Starting Era

E-sports originated in the 1970s with arcade game competitions, but quickly gained prominence in the late 1990s with the introduction of games such as “Quake” and “Starcraft.” The rise of the internet facilitated the growth of organized gaming tournaments, which allowed for worldwide access to these events. On top of this, the increasing popularity of games such as “Counter-Strike” and “Warcraft III” in the early 2000s began to draw increasingly large audiences and officially solidified e-sports as a legitimate form of competitive entertainment.

The beginning of the early 2010s marked a significant turning point for e-sports. The e-sports industry exponentially grew in viewership on a global scale which in turn resulted in more sponsorships and prize pools for competitors (largely due to the rise in popularity of games such as “League of Legends,” “Dota 2,” and “Overwatch''). With millions tuning in online and in-person events, e-sports organizations began to emerge, professionalizing the scene and creating career opportunities for those interested in joining franchised leagues.

In recent years, e-sports has continued to expand and diversify itself throughout different communities, platforms, and game genres. With a larger audience than ever that is still growing on a large scale, e-sports continues to attract investments from celebrities, corporate sponsors and traditional sports organizations. E-sports have ultimately solidified themselves into mainstream culture, demonstrating a worldwide desire for competitive gaming entertainment.
